# LinkedIn Voice Corpus Seed — 2026-03-11

Purpose
This file starts a few-shot corpus for what a genuinely non-AI, non-smoothed-out Callias writing voice should feel like on LinkedIn.

Working rule
The enemy is not just polish. It is statistical smoothness.
If the writing glides too cleanly, resolves too neatly, or sounds like a finished “insight post,” it fails.

What we are looking for instead
- rougher edges in a good way
- lived-in phrasing
- unevenness that feels human
- real opinion
- natural rhythm shifts
- slightly strange but believable turns of phrase
- less “content”, more actual thought

What to avoid
- em dashes
- balanced thesis / antithesis structure that lands too neatly
- generic leadership language
- clean lesson-post cadence
- stock transitions
- polished AI tone

## Version A
Maybe this is obvious, I don’t know, but I think a lot of teams are aiming at the wrong thing.

They say they want the team “using AI more.”

That already sounds off to me.

Nobody wakes up wanting to use AI more. They want some annoying part of work to stop eating an hour and a half for no good reason.

That’s the real thing.

Not:
we did a workshop
we made a prompt library
three people are into it now
somebody in ops is “driving adoption”

I mean more basic than that.

Did proposal drafting get less irritating?
Did weekly updates stop being this weird little tax on everyone’s attention?
Did follow-up stop depending on whoever feels the most guilty that day?

That’s the part I care about.

And honestly a lot of “AI rollout” still feels upside down to me. Too broad, too ceremonial, too much talk about transformation when the team would be thrilled if just one recurring task got less stupid.

That is usually the opening.
Not transformation. Relief.

## Version B
I think a lot of teams are using the wrong scoreboard.

They ask, are we using AI enough?

I don’t think that is the question.

The question is more like: did anything get less annoying?

Seriously.

Did someone stop rewriting the same internal update from scratch every week?
Did the handoff get cleaner?
Did the proposal start from somewhere better than a blank page and a low-grade sense of dread?

That’s the stuff.

Because right now a lot of companies have what I’d call ambient AI.
People poke at tools. Somebody has a few good prompts. There’s a lot of “we should probably do more with this.”

And then the actual workday shows up and everything snaps back to normal.

So if I’m looking at whether this is real or not, I’m not looking for excitement. I’m looking for friction reduction. Slightly boring, very real, repeatable friction reduction.

That’s usually when I start paying attention.

## Version C
I have a mild allergy to the phrase “AI adoption.”

Not because it’s wrong exactly. It just sort of hides the real question.

The real question is whether work got lighter anywhere that matters.

That’s it.

Not whether the team experimented.
Not whether somebody made a Loom about prompting.
Not whether leadership said the company is leaning into AI.

I mean, on a random Tuesday, did anything actually get easier?

Did client reporting get faster.
Did follow-up get less sloppy.
Did somebody stop carrying the whole memory of a workflow around in their head like a cursed family recipe.

That’s the line for me.

A lot of this stuff gets presented like the company needs a grand AI strategy. Maybe. Sometimes. But most teams I see do not need a grand strategy first. They need one repeated piece of work to stop being so weirdly manual.

Then people trust it.
Then it spreads.
Then you have something real.

Until then it’s mostly vibes.

---

## Version D
I keep thinking the target is smaller than people want it to be.

Everybody wants “AI transformation,” which, fine, sounds important.

But most teams would get more value from one thing quietly becoming less of a pain in the ass.

One thing.
Not twelve.

A weekly update.
A follow-up loop.
Proposal drafting.
Internal research that currently starts with six tabs and a headache.

That’s why a lot of AI talk feels fake to me. It jumps straight to adoption and culture and enablement and whatever else before answering the only question I really care about:

what got easier?

If the answer is basically nothing yet, then okay, we’re still at the demo stage. Which is fine. But let’s call it that.

The real shift starts when a team picks one recurring point of friction and actually fixes it in the workflow, not in theory.

That’s when people stop rolling their eyes.
